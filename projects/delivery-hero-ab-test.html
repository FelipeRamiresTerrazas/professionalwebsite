Delivery Hero: A/B Testing ETA Intelligence to Protect Margin and Trust

This project evaluates whether Delivery Hero should replace a distance-only ETA baseline with an ML-driven policy that treats kitchen load as a first-class signal. The test covers 10,000 sessions and isolates the business case across three dimensions: customer trust (cancellation rate), operational cost (support tickets), and revenue guardrail (average order value).

Business Context

Food delivery customers do not cancel because a rider is late. They cancel when the platform breaks a promise. ETA is a trust contract: when it is systematically wrong, the damage compounds across three channels simultaneously — customer abandonment, wasted kitchen preparation, and support ticket volume.

The core question for product leadership is whether ETA should remain simple and optimistic (distance-only) or become context-aware (ML + kitchen load). The financial stakes are high on both sides. Optimistic ETA is cheap to compute but destroys repeat rates. Calibrated ETA is operationally heavier but protects GMV and support cost structure.

  1. Customer trust KPI: cancellation probability under delay pressure.
  2. Ops cost KPI: support ticket rate driven by expectation mismatch.
  3. Revenue guardrail: average order value stability across variants.
Experiment base: 10,000 sessions (Control A = 4,996 · Treatment B = 5,004) with full fulfillment and behavioral outcomes tracked per session.

Experiment Design

The test was structured as a clean two-arm experiment with pre-defined guardrails. Variant assignment was at the session level; both arms operated against identical restaurant supply, rider availability, and demand conditions.

Dimension Control A Treatment B
ETA policy Distance-only baseline. Simple linear formula, no operational signals. ML-driven policy. Includes kitchen load, historical delay, and time-of-day.
Sample size 4,996 sessions 5,004 sessions
Primary metric Cancellation rate — target: reduce
Secondary metric Support ticket rate — target: reduce
Guardrail metric Average order value — must not degrade significantly
Cancellation model Logistic Regression (AUC 0.940) & Random Forest (AUC 0.951)

The guardrail constraint matters here: showing users a longer but accurate ETA may cause some to downgrade order size. The -4.33% AOV delta observed in this experiment falls within an acceptable band for initial rollout, provided margin dashboards are instrumented from day one.

Average order value shifted −4.33% in Treatment B versus Control A. This is the only adverse signal in the experiment. The hypothesis is that users shown a longer but accurate ETA occasionally downgrade order size — a tolerable tradeoff when cancellation and support savings are this large. Mandatory: instrument margin dashboards from rollout day one.

Business Impact

What the data shows: Variant A produces 33.8% cancellation and 45.4% support-ticket rate. Variant B produces 0.0% cancellation and 1.6% support. These are not incremental improvements — they represent near-elimination of two of the highest-cost per-order outcomes in food delivery operations.

Business scale interpretation: A platform processing 1M orders/month at a 34% cancellation rate converts approximately 340,000 canceled orders into completed ones under Treatment B conditions. The corresponding GMV, kitchen utilization, and support cost recovery is substantial enough to justify aggressive deployment.

The Patience Threshold

What the data shows: The inflection appears at 16 minutes delayed, where smoothed cancellation probability reaches 49.0%. Beyond this threshold, aggregate cancellation climbs to 74.8%. Below 10 minutes of delay, the rate remains below 20%.

What it means: User patience is not a gradient — it is a cliff. The risk regime is non-linear, which means the intervention window is narrow and visible. A platform that can predict 16-minute delay scenarios in advance has a viable retention response playbook.

Action this enables: Trigger automated interventions at 12–14 minutes predicted delay — proactive ETA update, compensation framing, and operational escalation — before crossing the cancellation cliff, not after abandonment intent appears.

The Kitchen Bottleneck

What the data shows: Median actual delivery rises from 33 minutes at low load (≤8 active orders) to 61 minutes at high load (≥20), a 1.85× inflation. This operational regime is entirely invisible to a distance-only ETA model.

What it means: Kitchen load is the central latency multiplier in the delivery system. A naive ETA formula that ignores it creates systematic expectation debt during peak periods — precisely when volume and cancellation risk are highest.

Action this enables: Introduce dynamic ETA inflation and restaurant throttling rules when active_orders_in_kitchen crosses saturation bands (15+, 20+) to prevent systemic SLA breakage during demand peaks.

Model Accuracy vs Naive ETA

What the data shows: Random Forest MAE is 3.91 minutes versus 12.61 minutes for the naive ETA, a 68.96% reduction in absolute error. The naive ETA mean bias is −10.55 minutes (systematically optimistic), while the RF model is near-unbiased at +0.03 minutes.

Why bias matters more than MAE: The naive model does not just have larger errors — it has directional errors that systematically under-promise on actual delivery time. This optimistic bias is the root cause of the trust collapse in Variant A: the platform consistently commits to ETAs it cannot keep.

Action this enables: Productize ETA via model-serving with real-time feature hydration. Track signed error bias as a first-class production KPI alongside MAE — it is more sensitive to trust degradation and catches regression before cancellation rates spike.

Cancellation Risk Drivers

What the data shows: The dominant cancellation driver is delay_min (importance: 0.430), followed by active_orders_in_kitchen (0.153), then ETA policy-related signals (variant indicators and eta_shown_min). The top three features explain the majority of the model’s discrimination power.

What it means: Cancellation risk is operationally explainable — and the main drivers are controllable platform levers. This is not random churn: it is predictable abandonment caused by specific, addressable supply-chain states.

Action this enables: Build a real-time session-level cancellation risk score using delay, kitchen load, and ETA discrepancy signals. Route high-risk sessions into retention treatment (proactive messaging, compensation, rider reallocation) before abandonment, not after.

Decision & Rollout

The experiment result is directionally clear: Treatment B eliminates the two highest-cost per-order outcomes in the test window. The rollout decision should be fast-track with structured risk monitoring, not a wait-and-see hold.

Recommendation: Roll out Treatment B in city-cohort phases with unit-economics instrumentation from day one.

Rollout Risks & Mitigations

AOV erosion at scale
Longer ETAs may shift order composition toward cheaper items. Mitigate: Monitor AOV by cuisine category and restaurant tier. A/B test ETA messaging framing to reduce perceived wait time impact.
ETA model bias drift in production
Kitchen load patterns shift seasonally and by city growth stage. Mitigate: Weekly signed-bias audit, monthly model refresh cadence tied to operational dashboards.
Intervention fatigue at delay threshold
Over-messaging users at the 16-minute checkpoint degrades trust in a different way. Mitigate: A/B test intervention copy and compensation framing independently of the ETA policy itself.
Restaurant partner friction
Kitchen load throttling rules may conflict with partner revenue maximization. Mitigate: Frame caps as SLA protection with GMV data. Pilot with high-performing partners first.

Follow-On Experiments

  • ETA communication format: test live countdown vs point-estimate vs range-estimate delivery for trust perception.
  • Intervention timing: trigger proactive messaging at 10 vs 14 vs 16 minutes to find optimal window.
  • Kitchen throttle policy: test automatic order caps at 15+ active orders versus operator-visible load alerts.
  • 90-day LTV delta: measure repeat rate and lifetime spend for users who experienced Treatment B versus Control A.
