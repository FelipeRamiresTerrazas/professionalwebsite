Delivery Hero: ML-Driven ETA A/B Test to Cut Cancellations and Support Load

This case study evaluates a controlled A/B test where Variant A used a naive distance-based ETA while Variant B used a predictive ETA model combining route distance and live kitchen congestion. The analysis quantifies impact on cancellations, support tickets, and delivery-time prediction quality, then translates the result into concrete product recommendations for production rollout.

The Business Problem

Late deliveries are one of the most expensive trust failures in food delivery. When ETA is consistently under-estimated, users perceive the experience as unreliable, which increases abandonment, cancellations, and support contact volume.

For product leadership, the strategic question is simple: should we keep the lightweight ETA logic (Variant A), or move to the predictive ETA model (Variant B) that incorporates both travel distance and kitchen load?

  1. Customer trust: ETA error directly impacts user confidence and conversion.
  2. Operational cost: delays increase cancellation and support ticket burden.
  3. Revenue safety: improvements must not cannibalize order value (guardrail KPI).
Dataset: 10,000 sessions, evenly split across variants (A=4,996; B=5,004), with behavioral and fulfillment outcomes.

A/B Test Setup & Guardrail Metrics

Variant A relied on a naive ETA approximation. Variant B used a predictive model calibrated on route distance and real-time kitchen pressure. The experiment was assessed on primary outcomes (cancellation and support ticket rates) and a business guardrail (average order value).

Machine Learning Models (Regression & Classification)

Two modeling tracks were run. First, a regression task predicted actual delivery time. Second, a classification task estimated cancellation probability using delay-aware features.

Regression (Delivery Time)

RandomForestRegressor and GradientBoostingRegressor trained on variant, rating, distance, kitchen load, and promised ETA.

Classification (Cancellation)

LogisticRegression and RandomForestClassifier trained with delay-based features to estimate cancellation risk.

Key Non-Linearity Test

Model comparison specifically evaluated MAE under high kitchen pressure to detect non-linear saturation effects.

Interpretability

Tree-based feature importance extracted to identify the strongest operational drivers of failure.

The delay-risk relationship is sharp: in low-delay buckets (early to up to 5 minutes late), cancellation was effectively 0%. In high-delay buckets (20+ minutes late), cancellation rose to ~75%. This demonstrates why ETA precision is not just a UX nicety, but a hard business lever.

Results & Impact on UX

The visual evidence confirms that Variant B is both more accurate and more operationally stable. Actual delivery times cluster closer to promised ETA, reducing customer frustration moments that typically trigger cancellations and support interactions.

Executive readout: the experiment indicates a strong quality lift from Variant B, driven by better handling of kitchen-load non-linearity while keeping average order value stable.

Executive Recommendations

1. Roll Out Variant B with Regional Phasing

The effect size on cancellation and support burden is large enough to justify rollout. Deploy first in medium/high-volume markets where kitchen pressure is frequently elevated and gains will materialize fastest.

2. Add Delay-Based Intervention Triggers

Introduce proactive UX and ops interventions when predicted delay exceeds 15 minutes: dynamic ETA updates, user reassurance messaging, and dispatch/kitchen escalation. This is where cancellation probability accelerates.

3. Track Guardrail and Unit Economics Weekly

Keep order value, refund rates, and discount compensation under active monitoring to ensure the ETA-model uplift remains net-positive on margin after full rollout.

4. Productionize with Ongoing Retraining

Kitchen dynamics drift by season, city, and campaign cadence. Set a retraining cadence and monitor model drift to preserve ETA accuracy over time.
