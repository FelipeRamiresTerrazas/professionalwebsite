Delivery Hero: A/B Testing ETA Intelligence to Protect Margin and Trust

This project evaluates whether Delivery Hero should replace a distance-only ETA baseline (Variant A) with an ML-driven ETA policy (Variant B) that includes kitchen load as a first-class signal. The goal is not just better prediction: it is reducing cancellations, lowering support burden, and preserving unit economics through expectation management.

The Business Problem

Food delivery customers do not cancel only because a rider arrives late. They cancel when the platform breaks a promise. ETA is therefore a trust contract, and trust failure has immediate financial and operational consequences: canceled GMV, wasted prep effort, and support ticket load.

The strategic question for product leadership is whether ETA should remain simple and optimistic (distance-driven) or become context-aware and behavior-aware. This A/B test isolates that decision.

  1. Customer trust KPI: cancellation probability under delay pressure.
  2. Ops cost KPI: support ticket rate caused by expectation mismatch.
  3. Revenue guardrail: average order value stability across variants.
Experiment base: 10,000 sessions (A=4,996 and B=5,004) with fulfillment and customer behavior outcomes.

A/B Setup & Guardrails

Variant A used a naive ETA baseline. Variant B used an ML-driven ETA policy incorporating live operational load. The test evaluated business outcomes and model quality with explicit guardrails.

The guardrail result is directionally acceptable for a product rollout decision: while B is slightly lower in average order value (-4.33%), the reduction in cancellation and support burden is large enough to justify controlled deployment and margin follow-up monitoring.

1. The Business Impact

Data Reality: Variant A shows 33.8% cancellation and 45.4% support-ticket rate, while Variant B shows 0.0% cancellation and 1.6% support. Net reduction is 33.8 percentage points in cancellations and 43.7 points in support burden.

Product Insight: ETA calibration quality is not cosmetic. It is a direct operating lever that changes customer behavior and support cost structure at scale.

Prescriptive Action: Roll out Variant B in phases by city cohort, but instrument unit-economics dashboards (refunds, coupons, compensation) to ensure the observed operational gains convert to durable margin improvement.

2. The Patience Threshold

Data Reality: The inflection appears at 16 minutes delayed, where smoothed cancellation probability reaches 49.0%. Beyond this threshold, aggregate cancellation climbs to 74.8%.

Product Insight: This gives Delivery Hero a concrete behavioral SLA. The risk regime is not linear; the user patience curve has a sharp cliff.

Prescriptive Action: Trigger automated interventions at 12-14 minutes predicted delay: proactive ETA update, compensation framing, and operational escalation before crossing the 16-minute cancellation cliff.

3. The Kitchen Bottleneck

Data Reality: Median actual delivery rises from 33 minutes at low load (<=8 active orders) to 61 minutes at high load (>=20), a 1.85x inflation.

Product Insight: Kitchen load is the central latency multiplier. Distance-only ETA cannot explain this regime, which is why naive ETA creates expectation debt.

Prescriptive Action: Introduce dynamic ETA inflation and restaurant throttling rules when active_orders_in_kitchen crosses saturation bands (for example 15+ and 20+) to prevent systemic SLA breakage.

4. Model Accuracy vs Naive ETA

Data Reality: Random Forest MAE is 3.91 minutes versus 12.61 minutes for the naive ETA, a 68.96% reduction in absolute error. Naive ETA has strong optimistic bias (-10.55 min) while RF is nearly unbiased (+0.03 min).

Product Insight: The model does not just improve average error; it eliminates systematic under-promising/over-delivering asymmetry that destabilizes trust and drives support contacts.

Prescriptive Action: Productize ETA via model-serving with real-time feature hydration, and track signed-bias drift as a production KPI to avoid return to optimistic ETA behavior.

5. Feature Importance for Cancellations

Data Reality: The top cancellation driver is delay_min (0.430), then active_orders_in_kitchen (0.153), followed by ETA-policy related signals (variant indicators and eta_shown_min).

Product Insight: Cancellation is operationally explainable. The main risk drivers are controllable platform levers, not random noise.

Prescriptive Action: Use these features to launch a real-time cancellation risk score that powers proactive retention actions (messaging, compensation, rider reallocation) before customer abandonment.

Executive Recommendations

1. Roll Out ETA Model B with Risk-Gated Launch

Prioritize medium and high-density zones first, where kitchen saturation is frequent and impact is highest. Keep weekly checkpoints on cancellation, support, refund, and margin deltas.

2. Operationalize the 16-Minute Patience Threshold

Treat 16 delayed minutes as a hard UX threshold. Trigger intervention playbooks before the threshold is crossed, not after cancellation intent appears.

3. Build Kitchen-Saturation Policy into ETA Product Logic

When active kitchen load moves above critical bands, inflate ETA dynamically and cap incoming order flow where needed. This is the fastest lever to reduce trust breakage.

4. Launch a Real-Time Cancellation Early Warning Layer

Use delay, kitchen load, and ETA discrepancy signals to score session-level churn risk and route cases into retention treatment before order abandonment.

5. Track Bias, Not Only Accuracy

MAE can improve while trust still erodes if ETA is systematically optimistic. Add signed error bias to product scorecards and enforce alerting thresholds for bias drift.
